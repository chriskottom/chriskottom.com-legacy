Your tests are talking to you.

Can you trust what they're saying?

You've read more than your share of blog posts and books, and you've worked your way through tutorials and programming exercises - some of them more than once. All in your spare time, all in the name of calling yourself a real Ruby developer, all because you care about code and want yours to be awesome. You're willing to do the work. You give a damn.

And after all those late nights and mental gymnastics, you're still left with this sneaking suspicion that your tests aren't everything they should be. Everything you thought they would be. It took weeks of fiddling to get your testing stack working reasonably well, and you're still not sure you've got all the kinks worked out. Your coverage is only so-so, and you know that there are parts of your code base that you just couldn't figure out how to test. Running your whole test suite used to take almost no time at all, but now it takes up to a half-hour, and you've gotten into the habit of only running it twice a day. And now on top of all this, your favorite testing framework with the DSL that reads like English has released a new version that changes the way tests are written. Definitely more headaches than you signed up for.

Hey, it's not just you.

Testing is a big part of working on all types of Ruby and Rails projects - commercial, personal, and open source. It's something we spend a lot of time working on for a whole range of reasons, but at the end of the day, we just want our tests to work well, run fast, and act as a guide to our work. But all too often, the tools get in our way.

A lot of Ruby developers have been drawn to Minitest for just this reason: simplicity. By keeping the framework APIs minimal, developers have less mental overhead to cope with when writing tests. That means fewer non-Ruby-like incantations to be invoked within tests and fewer stylistic decisions to be made when writing them.

But it's not just the APIs that are minimized. The Minitest source is an order of magnitude smaller than that of RSpec which means less behind-the-scenes hocus pocus and fewer WTF moments while still providing both a Test::Unit-style syntax for writing tests in straight-up Ruby and an RSpec-style DSL for defining tests using a block style. As a commenter on Reddit said: 90% of the expressiveness, 10% of the magic.
